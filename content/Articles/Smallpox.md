An acute viral disease transmitted by droplets and person-to-person contact, humans are its only natural vector, it's ~3000 years old having spread as civilizations grew and connected, responsible for many millions of deaths throughout history with a fatality rate up to 30%, it's unique for being the only human infectious disease to have been officially eradicated, with the only other eradicated disease being Rinderpest (cattle plague), a viral disease of mainly cattle & buffalo that was eradicated in 2011.

Pathogen: A DNA virus, Orthopoxvirus variola (VARV) of the Poxviridae family, named after the latin word for "spotted".

Case Presentation

Smallpox has two main forms: variola major and variola minor.
- An incubation (non-infectious) period usually 12-14 days long (range 7-
17).
- Acute fever, malaise, prostration, headache, and backache before rash onset.
- Subsequent cessation of fever and development of a maculopapular rash starting on the face and forearms, then spreading to the trunk and legs (centrifugal distribution), evolving within 48 hours to deep, firm, round, and well-defined vesicles and later pustules, which may become umbilicated or confluent.
- Lesions also develop in oral and nasal mucous membranes, ulcerating very soon after formation.
- From 8-14 days after the onset of symptoms, the pustules form scabs which leave depressed depigmented scars upon healing.

History of Immunization
- Variolation: Sore/pustule material transfer to healthy individuals, causing a milder form of the disease and providing protection against acquiring smallpox, seen as early as 200 BCE.
- Edward Jenner, 1796: By observing that those who had previously caught cowpox did not catch smallpox, he pioneered vaccine inoculation, borrowing the latin word "vacca" meaning cow.
Vaccine Generations:
- 1st gen. vaccines were derived from inoculative animal tissue, these were the type used in the eradication campaign.
- 2nd gen. vaccines were made by way of cell cultures.
- 3rd gen. vaccines were further refined, they are safer & more attenuated.
- The vaccine initially held cowpox, which then got shifted to vaccinia, both being members of the same genus as smallpox (orthopoxvirus).
- Bifurcated needles were used for administration, being easier to use and more cost-effective.

Eradication Timeline
- 1967: An intense WHO eradication plan involving immunization & surveillance was initiated, set out to achieve mass vaccination of up to 80% of the population, followed by case-finding and ring vaccination of the infected's contacts.
- Somalia, 1977: Last known natural case.
- England, 1978: Last known death, due to an airborne lab leak.
- December 1979: An agreement was made to limit the laboratory presence of smallpox to 2 labs: The American CDC and the Russian
VECTOR institute.
- 1980: The WHO declares smallpox eradicated.
