For the longest time, certain tissues were believed to be fully immune-privileged, inaccessible to the immune system and relying on indirect methods for protection from pathogens. Examples of such were the eyes and the CNS, but with the reconsideration of Schlemm's canal as a lymphatic vessel, the privileged status of the CNS is brought into question. 

![](/images/CNS-Lymphatics/IMG_5202.gif)

Lymphatics have 2 main functions: waste elimination (solute exchange), and immune surveillance. Due to similarities in function, the glial cells of the brain and the CSF apparatus have been called a "glymphatic" system, with the CSF gathering waste, interstitial fluid (ISF), and antigens and shifting them to the dural venous sinuses through the arachnoid granulations.

CSF spaces act as buffer/quarantine zones, putting a step between systemic circulation and the CNS, Virchow-Robin (paravascular) spaces ensheath the entirety of the cerebral vascular tree, separating the glia limitans, made of astroglial endfeet, and the basal lamina of the tree. These spaces are where the exchanges between CSF and ISF occur.

![](/images/CNS-Lymphatics/IMG_5689.jpeg)

![](/images/CNS-Lymphatics/IMG_5690.jpeg)

Due to this model having several issues, like the fact that arachnoid granulations aren't commonly present before the age of 2, further investigations were done to conclude that the glymphatic system comprises only the first three segments of brain fluid transport, including periarterial CSF influx, interstitial solute movement, and efflux along the perivenous spaces and cranial and spinal nerves, with lymphatic uptake and drainage representing the fourth and final step in the clearance of brain interstitial solutes.

![](/images/CNS-Lymphatics/IMG_5691.jpeg)

The lymphatics mentioned here are meningeal lymphatic vessels (MLVs), which have been detected as two lymphatic systems:

- The ventral meningeal lymphatic system, which can be seen easily at the level of cranial foramina and around the cranial nerves (IX-XII).

- The dorsal system, which includes channels adjacent to the venous sinuses, jugular veins, and within the posterior aspect of the foramen magnum.

Both dorsal and ventral systems are similarly prominent in humans.


There are findings that support the model of dual absorption of CSF, with arachnoid granulations specialised for absorption of aqueous CSF components and dural lymphatics specialised for macromolecular clearance.


These lymphatics may be relevant to CNS pathologies linked to protein misfolding: Alzheimer's , Huntington's, Parkinson's, etc.

Miscellaneous info I learned while reasearching this:

- Glymphatic function is dependent on astroglial aquaporin-4 channels.

- "Meckel's cave" is CSF-filled dural/meningeal pouch housing the trigeminal ganglion.

- "Neuropil": the intertwined network of axons, dendrites, and glial cells found in the gray matter of the central nervous system; Nervous tissue mass where the cellular processes dominate.
